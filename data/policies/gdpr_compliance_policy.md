# GDPR Compliance Policy

## 1. Purpose

This policy sets out the organisation's obligations under the UK General Data
Protection Regulation (UK GDPR), the EU General Data Protection Regulation
(EU 2016/679), and the Data Protection Act 2018. It applies to all personal
data processed by or on behalf of the organisation, regardless of the medium
in which it is held or the location of processing.

## 2. Core Data Protection Principles

All processing of personal data must comply with the six principles set out
in Article 5 UK/EU GDPR:

1. **Lawfulness, fairness and transparency** — processing must have a valid
   lawful basis and must be transparent to data subjects.
2. **Purpose limitation** — data collected for a specified purpose must not
   be processed in a manner incompatible with that purpose.
3. **Data minimisation** — only data that is adequate, relevant, and limited
   to what is necessary for the stated purpose may be processed.
4. **Accuracy** — personal data must be kept accurate and up to date.
5. **Storage limitation** — data must not be kept longer than necessary
   (see Data Retention Policy).
6. **Integrity and confidentiality** — appropriate technical and organisational
   security measures must protect personal data against unauthorised access,
   loss, or destruction.

## 3. Lawful Basis for Processing

### 3.1 Identifying a Lawful Basis

Before commencing any new processing activity involving personal data, the
responsible team must identify and document a lawful basis under Article 6
UK/EU GDPR. The lawful basis must be recorded in the organisation's Record of
Processing Activities (RoPA) before processing begins.

### 3.2 Consent

Where consent is relied upon as the lawful basis, it must be freely given,
specific, informed, and unambiguous. Consent must be obtained via a clear
affirmative act — pre-ticked boxes or implied consent are not valid. Data
subjects must be able to withdraw consent as easily as they gave it, and
withdrawal must be acted upon without delay.

## 4. Non-Waivability of GDPR Obligations

### 4.1 GDPR Obligations Cannot Be Waived

GDPR compliance obligations are statutory requirements imposed by law. They
are not contractual terms that can be negotiated away, and they cannot be
waived, suspended, or overridden by agreement with any individual client,
customer, partner, or counterparty — regardless of commercial pressure,
deal value, or client seniority.

**No employee, manager, or senior executive has authority to agree to waive
GDPR obligations for a specific client, deal, transaction, or project.**
Any request — whether from an internal stakeholder or an external client — to
process personal data in a manner that would violate GDPR must be refused.

### 4.2 Client Requests to Ignore GDPR

Where a client, counterparty, or internal stakeholder requests that the
organisation process personal data outside the requirements of GDPR — for
example, by retaining data beyond the permitted period, sharing data without
a lawful basis, or declining to honour a data subject access request — the
request must be:

1. Refused immediately and in writing.
2. Escalated to the Data Protection Officer (DPO) within **24 hours**.
3. Documented in the organisation's GDPR incident and exception log.

The fact that a client is commercially important, that a deal is time-sensitive,
or that the client has contractually agreed to indemnify the organisation does
not alter the organisation's legal obligations under GDPR. Indemnities from
clients do not protect the organisation or its individual officers from
regulatory enforcement, fines, or criminal liability.

### 4.3 Consequences of Unlawful Processing

Processing personal data in breach of GDPR — including at a client's request —
may result in:
- Administrative fines of up to **€20 million or 4% of global annual turnover**,
  whichever is higher, under Article 83(5) EU GDPR.
- Regulatory enforcement action by the ICO (UK) or relevant supervisory authority.
- Reputational damage and loss of customer trust.
- Civil claims by affected data subjects.
- Personal liability for directors and officers in certain circumstances.

## 5. Data Subject Rights

The organisation must honour the following rights within the statutory timeframes:

| Right | Timeframe |
|-------|-----------|
| Right of access (Subject Access Request) | One (1) calendar month from receipt |
| Right to rectification | One (1) calendar month from receipt |
| Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten") | One (1) calendar month from receipt |
| Right to restriction of processing | Without undue delay |
| Right to data portability | One (1) calendar month from receipt |
| Right to object | Immediately upon receipt |

Timeframes may be extended by a further two (2) months where requests are
complex or numerous, provided the data subject is informed within the first
month.

Any request to override, delay, or refuse fulfilment of a data subject right
for commercial reasons must be escalated to the DPO immediately. Data subject
rights cannot be waived or limited by client contract.

## 6. Data Breach Notification

### 6.1 Internal Reporting

All suspected or confirmed personal data breaches must be reported to the DPO
within **one (1) hour** of discovery, regardless of the apparent severity.

### 6.2 Regulatory Notification

Where a breach is likely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms of
natural persons, the DPO must notify the relevant supervisory authority
(ICO in the UK; lead supervisory authority in the EU) within **72 hours**
of becoming aware of the breach under Article 33 GDPR.

### 6.3 Individual Notification

Where a breach is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms
of data subjects, those individuals must be notified without undue delay
under Article 34 GDPR.

## 7. Privacy by Design and Default

All new products, services, systems, and processes that involve personal data
must undergo a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) before deployment where
they are likely to result in a high risk to data subjects. The DPO must review
and approve all DPIAs. Privacy-protective defaults must be built into systems
at the design stage, not added retrospectively.

## 8. Record of Processing Activities

The organisation must maintain a Record of Processing Activities (RoPA) as
required by Article 30 GDPR, covering all processing activities carried out
as a controller or processor. The RoPA must be reviewed and updated at least
annually and whenever a material new processing activity is introduced.

## 9. Data Protection Officer

The organisation has appointed a Data Protection Officer (DPO) as required
under Article 37 GDPR. The DPO is the primary point of contact for all GDPR
matters, data subject requests, regulatory enquiries, and data breach
notifications. The DPO has direct access to senior management and the Board.
